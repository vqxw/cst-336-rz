Using CS to Reverse Paralysis

Home How It's Done Process Result

In order to try out this experiment, George Courtine, a neuroscientist, and his team
used monkeys to test on.

They sliced through the monkey's spinal cord which paralyzed its right leg.

Afterwards, they installed two wireless sensors:
A recording device underneath the skull and a nerve simulator made up of electrodes
around the spinal cord to bridge the communication.

The implants worked together to transmit nerve signals between the brain and spine.

The first implant picks up brain signals which tells the muscles in the legs to walk.
The second implant unleashes electricity pulses, depending on the commands
from the computer and brain, to imitate nerves that operate muscles within the legs
to move and flex, which would lead to walking.

The brain signals are transferred to a computer wirelessly that decodes them
and sends a message to the second sensor (near the spine).

Because of the implants, the brain signals were able to go over the point of
injury in the spine.

Since there is now a path between the brain and spine, the brain signals can enter
the spine and the nerve simulations can activate the legs of the monkeys.
